8. Cross-Encoder (BERT) vs Bi-Encoder (SBERT)
Cross-Encoder / BERT
Slow but accurate
Sentence A + Sentence B → BERT → [CLS] → FeedFwd. → Score
Sentence A: Embedding S
Sentence B: FeedFwd.
Bi-Encoder / SBERT (Siamese)
Fast & scalable
Sentence A → BERT (shared weights) → Pooling (mean / [CLS])
Sentence B → BERT (shared weights) → Pooling (mean / [CLS])
→ Cosine Similarity
Bi-encoders scale to millions of comparisons!

12. Loss Functions
Cosine Similarity Loss
For similarity scores (0 to 1)
Minimize: [cos(a, b) – label]²
A
→ 0.85 ←
P
  • Intuitive
  • Works with soft similarity scores
  • Good for STS tasks
Multiple Negatives Ranking Loss (InfoNCE / NTXent)
For positive pairs + in-batch negatives
Maximize: s(a, p) > s(a, n₁), s(a, n₂), …
Anchor (a) → Positive + Negatives (N₁, N₂…)
  • Requires large batch
  • Strong performance
  • Most commonly used

17. Key Takeaways
  • Embeddings turn text into meaningful numbers.
  • Contrastive learning is key to learning similarity.
  • Loss functions & data quality matter most.
  • Pretrained models + fine-tuning > training from scratch.
  • Evaluate well — don't trust just one metric.
18. Best Practices
  • Use strong pretrained models
  • Choose the right loss (Cosine / MNR / TSDAE)
  • Use hard negatives when possible
  • Evaluate on STSB + MTEB
  • Monitor training & iterate
